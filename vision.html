Why this, and why now

Playing more barely moves the needle. Reviewing does.

A 30-minute game gives you three or four decisions that actually teach, and one bit of feedback: the result. The leverage isn't more games — it's density and retrieval, the two things a game can't give you.

Rating points gained per hour · 44,213 players (Southwick et al., 2026)

Game review4.41
Playing games0.86
Tactics puzzles0.73

So we don't build a puzzle grinder. We build the review — and let playing be the way you generate the material to review.

Puzzles, as most people use them, are statistically no better than just playing. The gains live in guided review and lessons — the parts every other trainer under-invests in.

The core idea

Organized by the kind of decision a position demands — not by opening, middlegame, endgame.

Phase is a book convention. What actually changes how you should think is the decision type in front of you. Each type gets its own task, its own scheduling, its own way of being graded.

Play it out

"Now convert it."

From the critical moment to the result, against a human-like engine.

The differentiator: the mixed queue hides the mode. Every other trainer announces it — "here's a puzzle, find the win" — so you already know a win exists. Real games never tell you. Noticing what kind of position you're in is the skill that separates 1200 from 2000, and it's the one place only a unified system can train it.

The loop

Study or play — every session ends in coached review.

First, master games: take the winner's side of a classic and commit at every move. Then your own games against a human-like opponent. Either way, you're never told the answer before you've committed to one.

How it's built

Two engines with two jobs, and an explainer that's never allowed to guess.

The machine owns every fact — legality, best move, evaluation. The language model only ever puts those facts into words.

The referee

Stockfish

Evaluates, verifies, grades. Never your opponent. Runs on your machine in the browser.

The opponent

Maia

A neural engine that plays like a human of a chosen rating — so you learn to punish the mistakes you'll actually face.

The explainer

Grounded LLM

Turns a bundle of computed facts into a plain-language "why." It renders and grades — it never evaluates the board itself.

chess.js + Stockfish → fact bundle (eval · win% · best line · hanging pieces · threats) → LLM explains & grades · never invents a move

Kept honest

What it trains — and what it can't.

Design elegance isn't evidence of results. The scope is stated plainly, and no metric is allowed to imply improvement it hasn't measured.

Trains well

  • Density & retrieval — many graded decisions per session
  • Noticing which kind of position you're in
  • Your own recurring leaks, from your own games
  • Positional judgment, traps, endgame technique

Won't pretend to train

  • Clock & time management
  • Deep calculation stamina
  • "Objectively worse but sets a trap" — engines misteach this
  • Real opening theory above ~1900

Estimated reach: ~75% of trainable skill at 1200–1600, ~65% at 1600–2000, eroding above. It's an asymptote to grow toward — never a plateau we'll claim to have reached.

The path

Content and the coaching loop first. The adaptive brain last.

The clever part — a model of exactly what you know — is worthless without the data the earlier releases generate. So it's built last, on purpose.

Adaptive

Non-negotiables

The rules that govern every later decision.

01Judgment, not memorized lines. Every item is "given this position, decide." Never recite theory.
02The mode stays hidden in the mixed queue — the one place mode-selection can be trained.
03Grade tiers, not rankings. Engine ordering within a tier is an artifact; don't teach it.
04Distractors come from humans — the natural move that quietly costs something, not the engine's fourth choice.
05A reason is required — and it's telemetry. Right feature, wrong move means a different fix than a guess.
06Play humans, grade with the engine. Maia to spar, Stockfish to referee — never the reverse.
07Content before adaptivity. Ship curricula that generate data; build the skill model on top of it.
08Be honest about the ceiling. No metric may imply transfer we haven't measured.
